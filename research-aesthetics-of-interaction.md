# Aesthetics of Interaction — Global Research Map

A reference document for the "Interfaces That Feel" project. Covers the major traditions, key figures, essential sources, and the theory-practice gap.

---

## The Field in One Paragraph

"Aesthetics of interaction" is the research territory at the intersection of interaction design, HCI, and phenomenology. The central question: what makes an interaction feel good — not visually, not functionally, but in the quality of the action itself? The field emerged primarily from TU Eindhoven in the early 2000s, has strong Nordic and Scandinavian branches, and connects to phenomenological philosophy (Merleau-Ponty, Heidegger, Dewey) and somaesthetics. Almost all work was done with bespoke physical prototypes. The translation to digital products at scale has never been systematically attempted. That's the gap.

## Traditions

### 1. Eindhoven School (TU/e) — The Origin

The most concentrated and theoretically coherent body of work. Produced the most operationalizable tools.

**Key figures:** Kees Overbeeke, Stephan Wensveen, Tom Djajadiningrat, Joep Frens, Caroline Hummels

**Core claim:** Aesthetic experience arises from the quality of the action-perception loop — the coupling between what you do and how the system responds — not from visual appearance alone.

**Key contributions:**
- **Feedforward** (Overbeeke) — communicating what *will* happen before the action, distinct from feedback (what happened after). Critical for making interactions feel coherent before you commit.
- **Interaction Frogger** (Wensveen, Djajadiningrat, Overbeeke — DIS 2004) — framework mapping how action and function are coupled through six characteristics: time, location, direction, dynamics, modality, expression. The most operationalizable tool the field has produced.
- **Rich Interaction** (Frens — PhD thesis 2006) — "aesthetics of coupling": the felt quality when form, interaction, and function are designed as an integral whole, not assembled separately.

**Essential reading:**
- Wensveen, Djajadiningrat & Overbeeke — "Interaction Frogger" (*DIS 2004*)
- Hummels & Overbeeke (eds.) — *International Journal of Design*, Vol. 4(2), 2010 (entire special issue)
- Petersen, Iversen, Krogh & Ludvigsen — "Aesthetic Interaction: A Pragmatist's Aesthetics" (*DIS 2004*)

### 2. Nordic / Scandinavian Tradition

**Key figures:** Lars Hallnäs, Johan Redström, Ramia Mazé, Jonas Löwgren, Marc Hassenzahl, Stoffel Kuenen

**Core claim:** As technology moves into everyday life, the design goal shifts from *use* (efficient task completion) to *presence* (how something occupies a life). Aesthetic interaction is interaction designed for presence, not just use.

**The use/presence axis** (Hallnäs & Redström) is the most useful conceptual frame for understanding why industry is stuck. Industry designs for use. The academic tradition is trying to add presence. Your project is about translating presence into something designable.

**Key contributions:**
- **Slow Technology** (Hallnäs & Redström, 2001) — founding text. Presence as an aesthetic category.
- **Pliability, rhythm, dramaturgical structure, fluency** (Löwgren) — the first named vocabulary of felt interaction qualities. Designable, reviewable.
- **Transitional theory** (Redström — *Making Design Theory*, MIT Press 2017) — theory that is deliberately unfinished, designed to be completed in practice. Directly relevant to bridging academic and industry discourse.
- **Temporal form** (Mazé — *Occupying Time*, 2007) — time as an aesthetic dimension of interaction; how interaction unfolds across time is itself a designable quality.
- **Experience design / hedonic quality** (Hassenzahl — *Experience Design*, Morgan & Claypool 2010) — the most industry-accessible Nordic voice. Pragmatic quality (what it does) vs. hedonic quality (what it is). Widely used in UX.
- **Aesthetics of Being Together** (Kuenen — PhD thesis, Umeå 2018) — extends aesthetics of interaction into social/collective territory. Structural mechanisms: Point of Summation, Inside-Out/Outside-In, Signal/Relation, Channel/Space/Thing.

### 3. Soma Design (KTH Stockholm)

**Key figure:** Kristina Höök (with Vasiliki Tsaknaki, Charles Windlin, Martin Jonsson)

**Core claim:** Designers must cultivate somaesthetic attention in themselves first — a felt, bodily sensitivity — before they can design for felt, embodied experience in others. The designer's body is a research instrument.

**Key contributions:**
- *Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design* (Höök, MIT Press 2018) — the current field statement. Theoretically rigorous, methodologically concrete, ends with a manifesto.
- "Felt Ethics" (*CHI 2023*) — extends soma methods into ethical design.
- "Change and Be Changed: Ten Years of the Soma Design Research Program" (*ACM TOCHI, 2025*) — retrospective. Explicitly states: making somatic methods accessible without a long apprenticeship is **the unsolved problem**. That's the gap you're occupying.

**Philosophical roots:** Richard Shusterman's somaesthetics — "the critical, meliorative study of the experience and use of one's body as a locus of sensory-aesthetic appreciation."

### 4. American HCI Tradition

More empirical, less phenomenologically rich than the European. Contributed foundational theory and several essential concept-level ideas.

**Key figures:** Paul Dourish, Don Norman, Mark Weiser, Erik Stolterman

**Key contributions:**
- *Where the Action Is: The Foundations of Embodied Interaction* (Dourish, MIT Press 2001) — bridge between Heidegger/Merleau-Ponty and mainstream HCI. Made phenomenology legible for interaction designers.
- *Emotional Design* (Norman, Basic Books 2004) — three levels: visceral, behavioral, reflective. Widely used in industry; operationally thinner than European frameworks.
- "Calm Technology" (Weiser & Brown, 1995) — ambient technology should move between center and periphery of attention without demanding conscious focus. Aesthetic ideal for ubiquitous computing.
- Kurosu & Kashimura — "Apparent Usability vs. Inherent Usability" (*CHI 1995*) — proved the aesthetic-usability effect empirically. The single most cited empirical result in this territory.

**Norman note:** His approach treats emotion as *response* (something that happens *to* the user). European tradition treats it as the quality of skilled embodied engagement (something that happens *in* the interaction itself). Meaningful theoretical difference.

---

### 5. Korean Research

**Key figure:** Youn-kyung Lim (KAIST)

**Key contribution:** "Interaction Gestalt and the Design of Aesthetic Interactions" (Lim, Stolterman et al. — *DPPI 2007*) — the holistic quality of an interaction that emerges from but transcends individual attributes. Close to what "felt qualities" are trying to name. Proposes attributes for designing aesthetic interactions.

---

### 6. Japanese Research

**Kansei Engineering** (Nagamachi, Hiroshima University, 1970s) — systematic methodology for translating customer emotional responses into product design parameters. Quantitative, data-driven, widely adopted by Sony, Toyota, Sharp. Less phenomenologically rich, more measurable. The most industry-integrated approach globally.

**Super Normal** (Fukasawa & Morrison, exhibition 2006) — practitioner articulation of *without thinking*: the best interactions leave no trace. An object becomes Super Normal through use.

---

### 7. UK / European

**Gaver (Goldsmiths)** — Ludic design: ambiguity as aesthetic resource. Technology for reflection and exploration, not task completion. *Drift Table*, cultural probes.

**Dunne & Raby (RCA)** — Critical design. *Hertzian Tales* (MIT Press, 2005). Electronic products have aesthetic properties beyond function. More speculative and critical than projective.

**Hekkert (TU Delft)** — Empirical product aesthetics. MAYA principle (Most Advanced Yet Acceptable). *Product Experience* (Schifferstein & Hekkert eds., Elsevier 2008) — most thorough empirical treatment.

## Adjacent Fields

**Philosophy:** Merleau-Ponty (*Phenomenology of Perception*, 1945) — the lived body, motor intentionality, tool transparency. Heidegger — ready-to-hand/unready-to-hand. Dewey (*Art as Experience*, 1934) — aesthetic experience as the quality of any fully realized experience, not just art.

**Feminist HCI:** Bardzell — "Feminist HCI" (*CHI 2010*). The standard interaction aesthetic embeds assumptions that are not neutral. Whose aesthetics are we theorizing?

**Crip HCI / Disability aesthetics:** Emerging since ~2019. Access as aesthetic experience, not just functional requirement. Disabled ways of knowing produce valid aesthetic frameworks.

## The Theory-Practice Gap — Summary

**What exists in academia:** Rich phenomenological frameworks (Eindhoven, soma), operational tools (Interaction Frogger, feedforward), experience vocabularies (pliability, rhythm, gestalt).

**What industry has:** Norman's three-level model (widely used, operationally thin), the aesthetic-usability effect (easy to cite), basic UX research methods.

**Why the gap persists:**
1. Most frameworks require either extensive philosophical background (to understand) or extensive somatic practice (to apply). Neither available on a 6-week sprint.
2. Almost all research done with bespoke physical prototypes. Never translated to digital product decisions.
3. No shared vocabulary for specifying feel as a design input. "Make it feel alive" fails as a brief.
4. No one owns the feel layer in a product team. It falls between designer and engineer.
5. No evaluation criteria. Products are reviewed on visual quality and task completion, not feel quality.

**Most promising bridges to practice:** Hassenzahl's experience design model, the Interaction Frogger, Lim & Stolterman's interaction gestalt, Kuenen's structural mechanisms.

**Höök's 2025 retrospective on soma design** explicitly names making somatic methods accessible without a long apprenticeship as the unsolved problem. That is the precise gap "Interfaces That Feel" can occupy.
